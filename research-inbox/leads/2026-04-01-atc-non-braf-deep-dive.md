# Non-BRAF ATC: immunotherapy, mutation-matched therapy, and trial leads

## Metadata

- Date: 2026-04-01
- Category: Treatment
- Priority: High
- Status: Queued
- Evidence level: Mixed
- Tags: ATC, non-BRAF, RAS-pathway, immunotherapy, trial, fusion-testing

## Research Question

For anaplastic thyroid carcinoma that is not clearly driven by classic BRAF V600E, what source-backed treatment and trial leads appear most worth discussing with the oncology team once exact molecular results and immediate safety constraints are clarified?

## Why This May Matter

This memo focuses on the clinically difficult bucket often described as "non-BRAF" ATC, which may actually include BRAF wild-type disease, non-V600 BRAF variants, RAS-pathway disease, fusion-driven disease, or tumor-agnostic immunotherapy biomarkers. The distinction matters because the evidence and treatment logic differ meaningfully across those groups.

## Key Findings

- Dual checkpoint blockade with nivolumab plus ipilimumab has prospective signal in ATC, with a 2024 phase 2 cohort reporting ORR 30% and clinical benefit rate 50% in a small exploratory ATC population.
- Mutation-matched targeted therapy plus checkpoint blockade appears more relevant when disease is RAS or NF related than when it is broadly "non-BRAF/RAS/NF," with a 2024 nonrandomized trial showing modest but clearer activity in the RAS/NF cohort than in the broader non-BRAF/RAS/NF cohort.
- Exact mutation class matters. A published case report of NRAS Q61R plus BRAF D594N ATC described complete pathological response to dabrafenib, trametinib, and PD-1 antibody after progression on prior therapy, suggesting some non-V600 BRAF settings may still justify targeted discussion.
- Fusion testing should remain in scope even if BRAF V600E is absent. Low-frequency but potentially actionable lanes include RET fusion-positive disease and NTRK fusion-positive disease.
- Trial leads specifically relevant to the non-BRAF bucket include NEO-COMBAT XL for BRAF V600E-wild-type ATC and the AIC100 CAR-T program, though recruitment logistics are limited.
- Anti-angiogenic strategies such as lenvatinib-based combinations remain important reference regimens, but published hemorrhage and fistula risk is a major caution in a patient context involving recent neck arterial rupture or similar local vessel threat.

## Relevance Assessment

This lead is highly relevant if the patient has confirmed ATC without a straightforward BRAF V600E-targeted lane, or if the exact molecular classification is still pending. It is especially relevant if the care team needs a structured way to think about next steps across several possible molecular buckets instead of treating "non-BRAF" as a single category. Relevance becomes stronger once the report clarifies exact BRAF and RAS variants, fusion status, and immunotherapy-related markers such as PD-L1, TMB, and MSI/MMR.

## Cautions And Limitations

- This is not a single treatment lead; it is a triage lead spanning multiple possible molecular scenarios, so next steps depend heavily on the exact pathology and molecular report.
- Several signals here come from small nonrandomized cohorts, case reports, or early-phase trials rather than large definitive studies.
- The lenvatinib and anti-angiogenic discussion has major safety caveats in the setting of threatened neck vessels, arterial rupture, wound-healing concerns, or fistula risk.
- Some referenced trials are active but not recruiting, closed to accrual, or geographically limited.
- "Non-BRAF" is often used imprecisely, and over-interpreting that shorthand before reviewing the actual report could send the workup down the wrong path.

## Suggested Follow-Up Questions

- Can the exact molecular report be reviewed line by line to determine whether this is truly BRAF wild-type, non-V600 BRAF, RAS-pathway disease, or fusion-driven disease?
- Was RNA fusion testing performed, and did it include RET, NTRK, ALK, and ROS1?
- Are PD-L1, TMB, MSI, and MMR available, and do they alter immunotherapy or tumor-agnostic treatment logic?
- Given the patient's local neck risk profile, which regimens are considered too dangerous from a hemorrhage, necrosis, fistula, or wound-healing standpoint?
- Is nivolumab plus ipilimumab still considered a viable discussion point once clinically safe, or is there a more appropriate mutation-matched or trial-based path?
- Should an ATC-focused referral or second-opinion review be used specifically for trial matching and molecular interpretation?
